Literature Survey

Sri Lanka's coral reefs are home to over 180 species of corals.[1]

Figure 1. coral increment and depletion 2005 - 2018 [1]

Coral reefs as complex structures and biodiverse and ecologically important marine structures are threatened by anthropogenic influences, climate, and pollution [4]. Good monitoring and assessment of coral reef ecosystems is very important for their conservation and management due to the increased complexity in their functioning. This system will analyze the present knowledge in understanding of the coral reefs, with special emphasis on recent methods and techniques in monitoring, assessment, and management

This system for coral conservation in Sri Lanka is another area of great concern where the conservation of the corals, foundational to marine life as well as the coastal people’s livelihood is at stake. This technology provides an analysis on how marine pollution impacts on these complex systems using soft computing and machine learning techniques. It uses Python, Firebase, Azure and React Native for analyzing the data and to enable real time monitoring.

Research Gap

Following areas are the research gaps found in most of the recent researches.

Identification & classification

There is a lack of comprehensive solutions for the identification and classification of seawater pollution, coral species, coral diseases, and marine life expectancy in Sri Lanka. Existing research has only focused on specific components, such as single coral species or general pollution levels, but lacks integrated approaches for diverse and real-world scenarios.

Severity assessment

Current methods for assessing the severity of marine pollution and coral disease impact are limited. Although some automated models exist, they struggle to quantify the progressive effects and risk levels associated with environmental changes and marine health degradation over time, which is essential for timely conservation efforts.

Information sharing

Real-time data sharing and communication are critical for stakeholders such as environmental agencies, researchers, and conservationists. However, a streamlined, effective system for sharing pollution levels, coral health, and marine life updates has yet to be developed, limiting collaborative action and quick response to emerging marine threats.

Research Problem & Solution

Proposed Problem

How to detect and assess marine pollution to protect coral ecosystems and biodiversity?

Marine ecosystems are increasingly threatened by pollution, which disrupts coral life and endangers marine biodiversity. These pollutants, including chemicals and waste, accumulate in seawater, leading to habitat degradation and a decrease in marine life expectancy. Coral reefs, essential to marine life and coastal protection, are particularly vulnerable, facing threats of disease and extinction as pollution levels rise.

Proposed Solution

Reefguard is an early warning system designed to protect coral ecosystems and marine biodiversity by leveraging advanced machine learning and computer vision techniques. It utilizes a Vision Transformer model to detect coral diseases, YOLO-V8 for identifying coral species, and a Random Forest model for assessing seawater pollution levels. Additionally, Reefguard combines Support Vector Machine (SVM) and Random Forest models to predict coral life expectancy. This integrated system generates real-time alerts for conservationists and stakeholders, providing actionable insights on pollution, disease, and coral health. With geolocation and mapping features, Reefguard enables timely interventions, supporting proactive marine conservation efforts.

Research Objectives

Seawater Pollution Identification

The first objective is to develop a reliable method for identifying seawater pollution levels in coastal areas. This includes analyzing specific pollution indicators and using a Random Forest model to assess water quality. By providing accurate and real-time data on pollution levels, the system will support early intervention to prevent further harm to marine ecosystems.

Coral Species Identification

The second objective is to create an automated system for identifying multiple coral species within complex reef structures. Using the YOLO-V8 model, the system will classify various coral species in high-density formations, facilitating better understanding and preservation of biodiversity in coral reefs.

Coral Disease Identification

The third objective is to enhance the early detection of coral diseases through image analysis using a Vision Transformer model. This model will accurately identify symptoms of coral diseases, helping conservationists to monitor and address disease outbreaks quickly, thus promoting coral health and resilience.

Marine Life Expectancy and Extinction Level Prediction

The fourth objective is to predict the life expectancy and extinction risk levels of marine species, with a specific focus on corals. By combining Support Vector Machine (SVM) and Random Forest models, the system will provide a comprehensive prediction of coral life expectancy, allowing stakeholders to prioritize conservation efforts for species at risk.

Methodology

Figure 2. High Level Architecture of the system.

The proposed Early Warning System consists of 4 main components. They are;

  1. Seawater Pollution Identification
  2. Coral Species Identification
  3. Coral Disease Identification
  4. Marine Life Expectancy and Extinction Level Prediction

Fig 2 illustrates the overall system architecture for Reefguard integrates Docker containerization and Azure Cloud services to provide a robust solution for marine ecosystem monitoring. Registered users can capture or upload images of coral reefs and input seawater quality data directly through the application. These inputs are processed within a Dockerized environment, where each model—Vision Transformer for disease identification, YOLO-V8 for species recognition, Random Forest for seawater pollution assessment, and the combination of SVM and Random Forest for life expectancy prediction—runs in isolated containers, ensuring scalability and efficient resource management.

The application’s backend is hosted on Azure, providing seamless access to cloud storage, real-time data processing, and secure infrastructure. Once uploaded, images are processed by the Vision Transformer model to identify and classify coral diseases accurately. Simultaneously, YOLO-V8 identifies multiple coral species in complex reef structures, enhancing the richness of biodiversity data. For seawater pollution detection, the Random Forest model evaluates key water quality parameters to provide real-time pollution levels. Finally, the system combines the outputs of SVM and Random Forest models to predict coral life expectancy, offering insights into potential risks to coral health.
